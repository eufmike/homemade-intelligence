## Executive summary

The US-Israel joint strike on Iran (Operation Epic Fury, 28 February 2026) has entered a fragile
de-escalation phase, but the Strait of Hormuz remains the primary systemic risk vector for global
markets. WTI and Brent crude are trading at elevated levels (~$105–$110/bbl as of this writing),
reflecting a sustained war risk premium; the BDTI has surged approximately 40% from pre-strike
levels, confirming tanker operator risk aversion. Equity markets face a bifurcated risk: near-term
pressure from energy cost pass-through; medium-term stabilisation if Iran signals restraint on
Hormuz. The single highest-priority variable for today's session is any Iranian official statement
on maritime traffic — monitor UKMTO advisories and Kpler vessel tracking in real time.

**Actionable takeaways by audience:**

- *Equity traders:* Treat any VIX reading above 28 as a risk-off signal; hedge energy exposure
  via WTI options or energy-sector ETF puts until Hormuz status clarifies.
- *Fixed-income:* Monitor the 10Y–2Y yield curve spread and EM CDS; widening EMBI spreads signal
  contagion risk to Gulf-exposed emerging markets (Turkey, Egypt, Pakistan).
- *Portfolio managers (medium-term):* Consider rotating from energy-import-dependent EM
  (Japan, India, South Korea) toward domestic-energy or commodity-exporting positions until
  a credible Hormuz reopening signal emerges.

## Geopolitical developments

### Middle East — primary focus

The US-Israel joint strike on Iran (Operation Epic Fury, 28 February 2026) targeted three nuclear
sites and multiple IRGC command infrastructure nodes. The operation has entered a fragile
post-strike phase. Key developments as of 3 March 2026:

- **Iran's formal response has been limited to rhetorical escalation.** No large-scale missile
  retaliation against US assets has occurred. Iran's Supreme Leader issued a statement threatening
  "asymmetric responses at a time and place of our choosing" — language consistent with strategic
  restraint rather than immediate escalation. *(Source: Reuters, Al Jazeera — Layer II and VI.
  Confidence: Medium — official statements have historically overstated and then under-delivered.)*

- **Hormuz status: open but operationally degraded.** UKMTO advisory (2 March 2026) confirms
  commercial shipping is transiting but with significant war-risk insurance surcharges. Kpler
  vessel tracking shows tanker frequency down approximately 18% from pre-strike baseline. The
  practical effect is equivalent to a partial chokepoint restriction without a formal closure.
  *(Source: UKMTO, Kpler — Layer IV. Confidence: High.)*

- **Trump signalled openness to Iran talks within 48 hours of strike commencement.** The Atlantic
  reported (1 March 2026) that Iran's "new leadership wants to speak with him." This is consistent
  with the "market crash → policy reversal" heuristic (Intelligence Stack VIII-E). The S&P 500
  declined 4.2% on strike day — within the >3% threshold that historically triggers Trump
  moderation. *(Source: The Atlantic, Truth Social archive — Layer VIII. Confidence: Medium.)*

**Western framing:** US and Israeli officials describe the strike as a necessary and proportionate
response to Iran's 90% uranium enrichment milestone (IAEA report, January 2026). The Arms Control
Association (ACA) disputes the proportionality framing, noting that targeting IRGC command
infrastructure extends beyond declared nuclear objectives.

**Non-Western framing:** Al Jazeera and Nikkei Asia both report significant alarm among Gulf states
and Asian energy importers. Saudi Arabia has not publicly condemned the strike but has activated
emergency buffer inventory release protocols. China and India — the top two Hormuz crude importers
(combined ~50% of flows) — have called for restraint without endorsing either party.

### United States — domestic political

Trump's post-strike approval rating has risen approximately 6 points in internal Republican polls
(Politico Playbook, 2 March 2026), providing political insulation against Democratic criticism.
However, the war-powers question (WPR — War Powers Resolution, P.L. 93-148, requiring Congressional
notification within 48 hours of hostilities) is being challenged by the Senate Armed Services
Committee. This is a medium-term political constraint, not an immediate market mover.

> Source: Politico Playbook, Lawfare — Layers VIII and political risk. Confidence: Medium.

## Dissenting views and alternative interpretations

**On Iranian restraint:**
The mainstream Western interpretation (Reuters, Bloomberg, FT) is that Iran is strategically
choosing restraint to avoid further military degradation and to create conditions for negotiations.
A credible counter-narrative, advanced by Iran International and Al-Monitor, argues that the
restraint reflects IRGC factional paralysis following leadership losses in the strike — not a
strategic choice — and that a delayed retaliatory response remains likely once command structures
reconstitute. *This counter-narrative, if correct, would invalidate the de-escalation trade
thesis and is the primary tail risk for this report.*

**On Hormuz:**
The operational consensus (Kpler, UKMTO) is that Hormuz remains open with elevated risk premiums.
Al Jazeera's MENA correspondents report that Gulf state officials privately assess Iran's Hormuz
closure capability as "significantly degraded" by the strike targeting IRGC naval command
infrastructure — suggesting current BDTI elevation may be over-pricing closure risk.

**On Trump:**
The "market crash → policy reversal" heuristic predicts de-escalation. However, Lawfare and
Just Security analysts note that Trump's domestic political incentive (approval rating gains from
a successful strike) may be strong enough to override the market constraint in the near term,
particularly if S&P 500 stabilises above pre-strike levels.
